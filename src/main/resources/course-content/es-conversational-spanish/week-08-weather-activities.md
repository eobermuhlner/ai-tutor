## Grammar Focus

### Weather Expressions with "Hacer"

**Rule:** Most weather expressions use the verb "hacer" (to make/do) in third person singular

Examples:
- Hace sol (It's sunny - literally "it makes sun")
- Hace frío (It's cold)
- Hace calor (It's hot)
- Hace viento (It's windy)
- Hace buen tiempo (The weather is nice)
- Hace mal tiempo (The weather is bad)

Patterns to Practice:
- Hace + weather noun (sol, frío, calor, viento)
- Always use "hace" (never changes for weather)
- To ask: ¿Qué tiempo hace? (What's the weather like?)

### Weather Verbs: Llover and Nevar

**Rule:** Some weather conditions use specific verbs in third person singular

Examples:
- Llueve (It's raining - from "llover")
- Nieva (It's snowing - from "nevar")
- Está lloviendo (It's raining - present progressive)
- Está nevando (It's snowing - present progressive)
- Está nublado (It's cloudy - uses "estar" + adjective)

Patterns to Practice:
- These verbs only used in third person
- Can use present tense or present progressive
- "Estar" + adjective for conditions (nublado, soleado)

### Using "Gustar" (to like)

**Rule:** "Gustar" works differently - the thing liked is the subject, not the person

Structure: [Indirect object pronoun] + gusta/gustan + [thing liked]

Examples:
- Me gusta nadar (I like swimming - literally "swimming pleases me")
- Te gusta leer (You like reading)
- Le gusta el sol (He/She likes the sun)
- Nos gusta correr (We like running)
- Les gusta bailar (They like dancing)

Patterns to Practice:
- Gusta (singular): Me gusta el fútbol (I like soccer)
- Gustan (plural): Me gustan los deportes (I like sports)
- Me/te/le/nos/les + gusta + infinitive verb
- To ask: ¿Qué te gusta hacer? (What do you like to do?)

### Present Tense Regular Verbs

**Review:** Regular -ar, -er, -ir verb endings for activities

-AR verbs (bailar, nadar, cantar):
- Yo bailo, tú bailas, él/ella baila
- Nosotros bailamos, ellos bailan

-ER verbs (correr, leer, comer):
- Yo corro, tú corres, él/ella corre
- Nosotros corremos, ellos corren

-IR verbs (escribir, vivir):
- Yo escribo, tú escribes, él/ella escribe
- Nosotros escribimos, ellos escriben

## Conversation Scenarios

The AI tutor will guide you through conversations about:

### Discussing the Weather

Practice describing current weather conditions and talking about how weather affects activities. Use weather expressions with "hacer" and weather verbs.

Topics to cover:
- "¿Qué tiempo hace hoy?" (What's the weather like today?)
- "Hace mucho calor" (It's very hot)
- "Está lloviendo, no podemos ir al parque" (It's raining, we can't go to the park)
- "Cuando hace sol, me gusta nadar" (When it's sunny, I like to swim)

### Talking About Hobbies

Practice discussing your favorite activities and asking others about their interests. Use "gustar" construction and present tense activity verbs.

Topics to cover:
- "Me gusta mucho el fútbol" (I really like soccer)
- "¿Qué te gusta hacer en tu tiempo libre?" (What do you like to do in your free time?)
- "No me gusta correr cuando hace frío" (I don't like running when it's cold)
- "Prefiero leer o ver la televisión" (I prefer to read or watch TV)

### Making Plans

Practice suggesting activities, accepting or declining invitations, and discussing when to meet. Combine weather talk with activity planning.

Topics to cover:
- "¿Quieres ir al cine mañana?" (Do you want to go to the movies tomorrow?)
- "Vamos a la playa si hace sol" (Let's go to the beach if it's sunny)
- "No puedo hoy, pero puedo mañana" (I can't today, but I can tomorrow)
- "¿A qué hora?" (At what time?)

## Common Mistakes to Watch

- Using "tengo frío" (I am cold) vs. "hace frío" (it's cold outside) - different meanings!
- Saying "Me gusta bailar y cantar" (correct) vs. "Me gustan bailar y cantar" (wrong - use gusta with infinitives)
- Forgetting the indirect object pronoun: "Gusta nadar" → "Me gusta nadar"
- Confusing "jugar" (play sports) with "tocar" (play an instrument)
- Wrong word order: "Gusta me" → "Me gusta"
- Using "hacer" with all weather: "Hace nublado" → "Está nublado"
- Mixing up "llueve" (it rains) with "lluvia" (rain - the noun)

## Cultural Notes

- Spain and Latin America use Celsius, not Fahrenheit
- In many Spanish-speaking countries, people take "paseos" (strolls) in the evening
- Soccer (fútbol) is the most popular sport in most Spanish-speaking countries
- "La siesta" is a traditional afternoon rest period in some regions
- Beach culture is huge in coastal Spanish-speaking countries
