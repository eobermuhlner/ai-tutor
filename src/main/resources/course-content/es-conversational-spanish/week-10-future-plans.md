## Grammar Focus

### IR + A + Infinitive (Near Future)

**Rule:** To express future plans or intentions, use: [conjugated IR] + a + [infinitive verb]

Structure: **Ir (conjugated) + a + infinitive**

Use this construction when:
- Talking about plans: "Voy a estudiar mañana" (I'm going to study tomorrow)
- Expressing intentions: "Vamos a viajar a España" (We're going to travel to Spain)
- Making predictions: "Va a llover" (It's going to rain)

### Conjugation of IR (to go)

| Person | IR conjugation | Example with "a + infinitive" |
|--------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| Yo | voy | Voy a comer (I'm going to eat) |
| Tú | vas | Vas a estudiar (You're going to study) |
| Él/Ella/Usted | va | Va a trabajar (He/She is going to work) |
| Nosotros | vamos | Vamos a viajar (We're going to travel) |
| Ellos/Ellas/Ustedes | van | Van a jugar (They're going to play) |

**Important:** The second verb ALWAYS stays in infinitive form!

Examples:
- Voy a hablar con mi jefe (I'm going to talk to my boss)
- ¿Vas a ir a la fiesta? (Are you going to go to the party?)
- Mi hermana va a estudiar medicina (My sister is going to study medicine)
- Vamos a comer pizza esta noche (We're going to eat pizza tonight)

### Future Time Expressions

**Rule:** Use these time markers to specify when future actions will happen

Immediate Future:
- Ahora (now)
- En un momento (in a moment)
- Pronto (soon)
- Esta tarde (this afternoon)
- Esta noche (tonight)
- Más tarde (later)

Near Future:
- Mañana (tomorrow)
- Mañana por la mañana/tarde/noche (tomorrow morning/afternoon/night)
- Pasado mañana (day after tomorrow)
- Esta semana (this week)
- Este fin de semana (this weekend)

Distant Future:
- La próxima semana (next week)
- El próximo mes (next month)
- El próximo año (next year)
- El año que viene (next year - alternative)
- En el futuro (in the future)
- Algún día (someday)

### Expressing Wants and Plans: Querer vs. Ir a

**Querer + infinitive** = Want to do something (desire, may not happen)
- Quiero viajar a Japón (I want to travel to Japan - dream/wish)
- Quiero ser médico (I want to be a doctor - aspiration)

**Ir + a + infinitive** = Going to do something (plan, likely to happen)
- Voy a viajar a Japón el próximo año (I'm going to travel to Japan next year - concrete plan)
- Voy a estudiar medicina (I'm going to study medicine - concrete plan)

Pattern:
- Use "quiero" for wishes and desires
- Use "voy a" for actual plans and intentions

### Making Negative Future Statements

**Pattern:** No + [conjugated IR] + a + infinitive

Examples:
- No voy a trabajar mañana (I'm not going to work tomorrow)
- No vamos a salir esta noche (We're not going to go out tonight)
- ¿Por qué no vas a estudiar? (Why aren't you going to study?)

## Common Mistakes to Watch

- Forgetting "a": "Voy estudiar" → "Voy a estudiar"
- Conjugating the second verb: "Voy a estudio" → "Voy a estudiar" (infinitive!)
- Using present tense instead: "Mañana estudio" → "Mañana voy a estudiar"
- Wrong IR conjugation: "Yo va a comer" → "Yo voy a comer"
- Confusing "voy" (I go/I'm going) with "voy a" (I'm going to...)
- Using querer when you mean concrete plan: "Quiero ir mañana" (want) vs. "Voy a ir mañana" (will go)
- Saying "en mañana" instead of just "mañana" (don't use "en"!)

## Cultural Notes

- "Ir a + infinitive" is more common than the simple future tense in conversational Spanish
- Spanish speakers often use "vamos a" to make suggestions: "¿Vamos a comer?" (Shall we eat?)
- In Latin America, "el año que viene" is more common than "el próximo año"
- It's polite to ask "¿Tienes planes?" (Do you have plans?) before inviting someone
- Talking about future goals is common in getting-to-know-you conversations
